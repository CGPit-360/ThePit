Designs of the week

Naive

a simple, colorful, and expressive style that embraces childlike perspectives and ignores traditional rules of realism and proportion.

Minimalism

a clean and simple design style that focuses on essential elements, using limited colors, space, and details to create clarity and balance.

Psychedelic

a vibrant and imaginative style characterized by bold colors, distorted shapes, swirling patterns, and surreal visuals that create a dreamlike or mind-bending effect.

Postmodernism

a bold and experimental design style that mixes different elements, embraces irony and playfulness, and breaks traditional design rules to create expressive and unconventional visuals.

Brutalism

a raw and striking design style characterized by heavy typography, stark contrasts, rigid grids, and an intentionally rough or unpolished appearance.

Swiss

a clean and structured design approach that emphasizes grid systems, sans-serif typography, clarity, and strong visual hierarchy.

HISTORY OF ART

It started with a handprint on a cave wall.

Before museums, before galleries, before names were signed in the corners of canvases, there was a human being pressing pigment against stone, leaving proof that they existed. From those ancient markings in places like Lascaux Cave, art began as survival, ritual, and storytelling.

Centuries later, the ceilings of Sistine Chapel bloomed with divine drama as Michelangelo painted stories across the sky of a church. Art became power, religion, science, and rebellion all at once.

Then came revolutions. Light fractured into color in the works of Claude Monet. Reality bent and broke under the brush of Pablo Picasso. Artists stopped asking, "How does it look?" and started asking, "How does it feel?"

And today, art lives everywhere — on screens, walls, clothes, streets. It glitches, loops, distorts, and reinvents itself. But at its core, it is still that first handprint: a human saying, I was here.

Art began with a handprint and became a voice for humanity.
